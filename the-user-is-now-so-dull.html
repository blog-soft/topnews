The user is now so dull

 

On YouTube, Facebook or Instagram, everyone has been an advertising medium for a decade. Nevertheless, there is still uncertainty on all sides about the legally compliant handling of what is now called German influencer marketing. That is why the German Council for Public Relations (DRPR) has revised its "Guideline for PR in digital media and networks". The sender of a post should always be transparent: Who paid for the post? Who comments on it and spreads it further? Manipulation through so-called social bots and fake news are prohibited. This influencer guideline is now followed by three advertising associations: the Organization of Media Agencies (OMA), the Association of Communication Agencies (GWA) and the Content Marketing Forum (CMF).
One who knows the uncertainty well is the Berlin fashion and lifestyle blogger Vreni Frost. At the end of May, the regional court in Berlin approved an injunction by the Social Competition Association (VSW). Frost had tagged brands and companies on Instagram in her posts without marking the posts as advertising. The blogger argued that she was not paid for it. Taggen sees it as an editorial act. Since then, Frost has also marked photos of her cat, potted plants and flower meadows as "advertising".
SZ: Do you think the influencer directive makes sense?
Vreni Frost: It's a step in the right direction, but frankly, I don't really understand what was decided. There are actually understandable and good laws, for example the Telemedia Act and the Law against Unfair Competition, UWG for short. Thereafter, journalistic and advertising content must be clearly separated and labeled. Things are touched on in the directive, but the real problems are not at all.
