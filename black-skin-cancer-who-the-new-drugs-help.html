Black skin cancer: who the new drugs help

The new checkpoint inhibitors can help melanoma sufferers, but not everyone. A study shows the limits of immunotherapy
There are many forms of skin cancer. Malignant melanoma is the form that is dangerous for the entire organism because it is the only skin tumor that can also form metastases. The so-called black skin cancer is particularly dangerous if it has already formed metastases in other organs.
But melanoma patients in particular have had hope in recent years with new drugs, the so-called immune checkpoint inhibitors, specifically PD1 blockers. There are patients whose lives have been significantly improved by these immunomodulating agents, which means that they did not die, but lived with their illness.
To explain: Some types of cancer produce a protein (PD-L1), which prevents the body's immune cells from recognizing cancer and fighting it effectively. Immune checkpoint inhibitors are able to block the PD-1 receptor so that PD-L1 no longer obstructs the immune cells. The cancer is therefore visible to the immune system and can be attacked.
Make predictions
In other melanoma patients, however, the immune checkpoint inhibitors worked much less well and it was therefore a matter of concern for medical professionals to be able to make predictions about the effectiveness. A study of unprecedented size combined clinical parameters from patients with advanced black skin cancer with extensive genetic analysis of the tumor tissue. One of the goals was to be able to predict the effectiveness of an immune checkpoint blockade individually for each patient.
